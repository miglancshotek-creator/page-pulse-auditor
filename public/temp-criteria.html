01/07 Value Proposition LIFT-V · Schwartz · Ogilvy 0/10 ▼
Is the core offer immediately clear, desirable, and differentiated? The value proposition is the single most important element — it determines whether the visitor decides to read further within the first 3 seconds. Most pages fail silently: the copy is accurate but not compelling, specific but not differentiated, or clear but not relevant to the visitor's actual emotional state.
1
Hero headline answers "what do you do?" in under 5 seconds
A stranger with zero context can state back what the company does after reading only the headline.
The 5-second test from LIFT: cover everything below the headline. Read it cold. If the answer to "what is this?" requires scrolling or guessing, this fails. The grunt test: could a caveman understand the offer? This is the most commonly failed criterion on paid traffic pages — companies write for people who already know them, not for strangers arriving from an ad. The brain makes a stay-or-go decision in under 3 seconds; the headline is the only copy evaluated in that window.
✓ Pass examples
"We audit your landing page across 70 criteria and show you exactly what it's costing you each month."
Clear: what (audit), scope (70 criteria), outcome (cost calculation) — all in one sentence.
"Turn your ad clicks into customers — without spending more on traffic."
Clear problem (wasted ad spend), clear outcome (more customers), clear mechanism (not more spend).
✗ Fail examples
"We help ambitious businesses grow."
Fails because it describes almost any company on earth. Zero specificity about what is actually done.
"Your partner in digital transformation."
Jargon that means nothing. "Digital transformation" is not an offer — it's a buzzword.
Critical
2
Headline leads with customer outcome, not company mechanism
The headline names what the visitor gets or avoids — not what the company does to deliver it.
StoryBrand principle: the customer is the hero, not the brand. Most companies write about themselves — their process, their team, their approach — because that's what they know. The visitor only cares about what changes for them. "We build landing pages" describes the company. "Your ads will finally convert" describes the visitor's new reality. The first creates mild interest; the second creates desire. Ogilvy tested this for decades: outcome headlines consistently outperform feature headlines.
✓ Pass — outcome-first
"Stop losing €3,000/month to a landing page that doesn't convert."
Loss-framed, specific number. The visitor is the subject of every sentence.
"Find out exactly why your ads aren't converting — in 20 minutes."
What the visitor receives (knowledge), and how quickly. Company is invisible.
✗ Fail — mechanism-first
"We build high-converting landing pages using data-driven CRO methodology."
Every sentence starts with "We." The visitor's outcome is implied but never stated directly.
"Our proven 7-step audit process identifies conversion bottlenecks."
What does the visitor get? Revenue increase? Time saved? Not stated anywhere.
Critical
3
Value proposition is visible above the fold without scrolling
The core offer — what, for whom, and why it matters — is readable on page load on both desktop and mobile.
The above-fold zone is the only section 100% of visitors see. On mobile, 50–80% of paid traffic visitors never scroll past the first screen. Test: load the page on a phone with the screen as it appears on first load. Can you read the full offer? Is there a CTA visible? If the answer is no to either, this fails. Common causes: hero image too tall pushing text below fold, large navigation consuming viewport space, hero video with no immediate text overlay.
✓ Pass
Headline + sub-headline + CTA button all visible on a 375px mobile viewport without touching the screen.
Full value stack in guaranteed viewport. No scrolling required to understand and act.
✗ Fail examples
Full-screen hero video plays for 8 seconds before text fades in. CTA appears at 12 seconds.
Visitor must wait 12 seconds before they can act. Most are gone within 3.
Large decorative hero image (600px tall on mobile) pushes headline below fold on all phone sizes.
The image contributes nothing to the value proposition but consumes the entire guaranteed viewport.
Critical
4
Headline passes the Bly 4 U's test: Useful, Ultra-specific, Unique, Urgent
Score each U independently. The headline must pass at least 3 of 4 to qualify as passing.
Each U adds independent persuasive force: Useful (does it promise something the visitor wants?), Ultra-specific (does it include a number, timeframe, or concrete detail?), Unique (does it differentiate from competitor headlines?), Urgent (does it give a reason to care now?). A headline doing only one job — say, only "Useful" — is leaving three persuasive levers unpulled. Test: score each U as 0 or 1, total. Score of 2 or below = rewrite.
✓ Pass — scores 4/4
"Stop losing €3,200/month to a landing page your visitors can't navigate in 5 seconds."
Useful ✓ (stop losing money) · Ultra-specific ✓ (€3,200/mo, 5 seconds) · Unique ✓ (navigation framing) · Urgent ✓ (happening right now)
✗ Fail — scores 1/4
"We help you grow your business online."
Useful ✓ (barely) · Ultra-specific ✗ · Unique ✗ (every agency says this) · Urgent ✗. Generic at maximum.
High
5
Sub-headline explains the unique mechanism in one sentence
The sentence directly below the headline explains how the outcome is delivered, differentiating from alternatives.
Google Ads traffic is almost always Schwartz Stage 3 (Solution Aware) — they know CRO agencies exist, they're evaluating which one. The headline catches attention; the sub-headline must answer "but why this one specifically?" "We audit 70 criteria, calculate the revenue cost of every issue, and walk you through it in 20 minutes" is a mechanism claim. Without it, two agencies with identical headlines are indistinguishable — and the visitor defaults to price comparison.
✓ Pass
"We audit your page across 70 criteria, calculate the monthly revenue you're losing on each issue, and walk you through the findings — free."
Mechanism (70-criteria audit), differentiator (revenue calculation per issue), offer (free walkthrough). All in one sentence.
✗ Fail examples
"We're a team of experienced conversion specialists dedicated to improving your results."
No mechanism. "Experienced" and "dedicated" are self-awards. "Results" is vague. This could describe any agency.
"Our data-driven approach helps businesses maximise their ROI."
"Data-driven" is claimed by 95% of agencies. "Maximise ROI" is an aspiration, not a mechanism.
High
6
The offer is differentiated from obvious alternatives
A visitor who has seen 2–3 competitors can immediately identify what makes this offer different.
The logo swap test: remove the company logo and replace it with a competitor's. Does the page still make perfect sense? If yes, differentiation has failed. Most landing pages in any category are interchangeable because they all describe the same features using the same vocabulary. The unique mechanism must be named explicitly, not implied. "We audit before we build, so every change is backed by data" differentiates from agencies that redesign first and measure later.
✓ Pass
"Unlike agencies that redesign first and measure later — we calculate the revenue cost of every issue before touching a single pixel."
Names the competitor behaviour explicitly, then contrasts with a specific differentiating action.
✗ Fail
"We're a full-service CRO agency with 10+ years of experience and a client-first approach."
Every agency claims years of experience and client-first approach. This describes the category, not the company.
High
7
No vague superlatives in headline or opening copy
Words like "world-class", "leading", "powerful", "best", "revolutionary", "innovative" are absent from the headline and first paragraph.
Ogilvy: "cleans 73% more effectively" is credible because the number implies testing. "Cleans better" is not. Vague superlatives actively reduce trust with sophisticated buyers — they signal "we couldn't find a specific claim." Every superlative is a missed opportunity for a specific one. Replace "world-class" with "70-criteria framework." Replace "best-in-class results" with "average CR improvement of 1.4 percentage points across client pages."
✓ Pass — specific claims
"We audit 70 criteria across 7 CRO frameworks — most agencies check fewer than 20."
Specific number (70), named scope (7 frameworks), competitive context. All verifiable.
✗ Fail — vague superlatives
"We deliver world-class, industry-leading conversion optimisation solutions using cutting-edge methodology."
Five vague superlatives in one sentence. Zero specific claims. This sentence could be removed entirely.
High
8
The hero section names who the offer is for
The target audience is explicitly identified — by role, situation, or pain — within the hero section.
Cialdini's Unity: "for businesses running paid traffic" tells the right person "this is for me" and tells the wrong person to self-select out — which is equally valuable. Pages that try to speak to everyone convert no one because generalised copy lacks the specificity that resonates with any individual. Audience clarity test: can you tell the visitor's job title, their current problem, and why they might be searching today?
✓ Pass
"For marketing teams spending €3k+ per month on paid ads and wondering why the conversions don't match."
Role (marketing teams), qualifying condition (€3k+ spend), emotional state (wondering why conversions don't match).
✗ Fail
"For businesses of all sizes looking to improve their online performance."
"All sizes" and "online performance" are maximally vague. Nobody feels specifically spoken to.
Medium
9
Copy uses loss framing at least once in the hero
At least one sentence in the hero frames around what the visitor is currently losing, not just what they could gain.
Kahneman's loss aversion: losses feel 2× more painful than equivalent gains. "Stop losing €3,000/month" triggers stronger emotional response than "earn €3,000/month more" even though the net outcome is identical. For paid traffic pages, this framing is especially powerful — the visitor is already spending money on ads, so framing their current situation as an ongoing loss is viscerally resonant in a way that gain-framing is not.
✓ Pass — loss-framed
"Every month your page underperforms, you're handing money to competitors who convert better."
Ongoing present-tense loss. Active competitor benefiting from the inaction. Creates urgency from reality.
"At 2% conversion rate, you're losing €2,400/month that a 3% page would keep."
Specific calculation turns abstract "underperformance" into a concrete monthly bill.
✗ Fail — pure gain framing
"Increase your conversion rate and unlock new revenue potential."
Purely gain-framed. "Unlock potential" is abstract and future-tense. Generates mild interest, not urgency.
Medium
10
The offer is concrete and specific, not categorical
The offer names a specific deliverable with a specific scope — not a vague service category.
Heath Brothers Concreteness: "70-criteria audit with revenue-leak calculation and 20-minute walkthrough, free" is sticky and memorable. "CRO services" is a category that disappears from memory within seconds. Concrete offers are remembered because the brain can form a specific mental image of them. Vague offers signal the company either hasn't thought it through or is deliberately hiding details to avoid commitment.
✓ Pass — concrete offer
"A 70-point audit of your landing page, a revenue-leak calculation showing the €/month cost of each issue, and a 20-minute video walkthrough — delivered within 5 business days."
Specific deliverables (3), specific format (video), specific timeframe (5 days). The visitor knows exactly what they get.
✗ Fail — categorical offer
"Comprehensive conversion rate optimisation services tailored to your business needs."
"Comprehensive" and "tailored" are category descriptors. The visitor has no idea what they actually receive.
Medium
02/07 Relevance & Message Match LIFT-R · Schwartz Awareness 0/10 ▼
Does the page match the ad, keyword, and intent of the arriving visitor? Message match accounts for roughly 40% of avoidable bounce rate on paid campaigns. A great page receiving mismatched traffic still fails. The visitor's brain is looking for confirmation they landed in the right place — any signal that they didn't triggers exit before a word of copy is read.
11
Page headline echoes the primary ad headline or keyword intent
The ad and page headline share at least one key phrase or directly address the same intent.
LIFT Relevance: the brain experiences a "scent" from ad to page. When the ad says "fix your landing page conversion rate" and the page headline says "We help businesses grow" — the scent breaks. The visitor's System 1 registers "wrong place" before any copy is processed. This happens in the first 200–300ms of page load, before the visitor has time to read anything. For paid traffic, the page exists only in the context of the ad that preceded it — it must honour that context explicitly.
✓ Pass — strong scent trail
Ad: "Landing page audit — find out what's killing your conversions" → Page headline: "Find out exactly what's killing your conversions — in 20 minutes."
Key phrase carried from ad to page. Visitor immediately feels confirmation: "yes, right place."
✗ Fail — broken scent
Ad: "CRO audit — see your revenue leak" → Page headline: "Welcome to Acme Agency. We build better digital experiences."
Zero language match. The ad promised a specific diagnostic; the page opens on a brand statement.
Critical
12
Page addresses Schwartz Stage 3 (Solution Aware) — the default for paid search
The page assumes the visitor knows the category exists and leads with differentiation, not category education.
Someone clicking a Google Ad for "CRO agency" is Stage 3: they know what CRO is, they know agencies exist, they are evaluating providers. A page that opens with "What is conversion rate optimisation?" wastes the most valuable screen real estate on explaining something the visitor already knows. Their actual question is "why this agency over the three other tabs I have open?"
✓ Pass — Stage 3 appropriate
"Most CRO audits give you a list of recommendations. Ours gives you a revenue number attached to every single issue — so you know exactly what to fix first."
Assumes the visitor knows what a CRO audit is. Differentiates on a specific mechanism vs. the assumed alternative.
✗ Fail — Stage 2 content for Stage 3 visitor
"What is conversion rate optimisation? CRO is the process of increasing the percentage of visitors who take a desired action..."
Definition content for someone who already knows the definition. Signals the agency doesn't know who they're talking to.
Critical
13
Page serves a single, defined audience — not multiple segments
The copy speaks to one visitor profile. No simultaneous targeting of e-commerce and SaaS, or startup and enterprise.
The specificity required to resonate deeply with one segment will feel irrelevant to another. A page trying to speak to both produces copy generic enough for neither. One traffic source → one audience → one page. Audience clarity test: read the first paragraph aloud — can you tell the visitor's job title, their current problem, and why they might be searching today?
✓ Pass — single audience
Entire page written for "marketing managers at e-commerce brands spending €3k–€30k/month on paid ads." Every example uses product pages and cart abandonment. Every metric references e-commerce benchmarks.
The right visitor reads this and thinks "they understand my world exactly."
✗ Fail — multi-segment dilution
"Whether you're an e-commerce store, a SaaS startup, a service business, or an enterprise brand — we help all types of businesses."
"All types" means being specific to none. Every "type" mentioned reduces the resonance with each individual type.
Critical
14
Page tone matches the sophistication level of the traffic source
Copy complexity, vocabulary, and assumed knowledge level match what the visitor brings from the ad or referral source.
A CMO with 15 years of experience clicking a LinkedIn ad and a first-time business owner clicking a Facebook ad arrive with completely different knowledge, vocabulary, and patience for complexity. Over-simplified copy insults the sophisticated buyer. Jargon-heavy copy loses the generalist. The test: read the copy aloud imagining the specific person who saw the specific ad. Does it feel written for them or about them from a distance?
✓ Pass — tone-matched
LinkedIn ad targeting CMOs → page uses "conversion funnel architecture," "ROAS degradation," "attribution modelling." Assumes deep knowledge, references industry benchmarks by name.
The CMO audience feels seen. The vocabulary signals peer-level expertise rather than a vendor pitching up.
✗ Fail — mismatch
Google Search Ad for "CRO agency" targeting CMOs → page opens with "Have you ever wondered why visitors leave your website without buying anything?"
Condescending to a CMO audience. Explaining that visitors leave without buying to someone whose job is conversion signals the agency doesn't know their audience.
High
15
Offer on the page matches the offer in the ad exactly
If the ad says "free audit," the page leads with a free audit. The offer is consistent from click to landing.
Bait-and-switch is the most common and most damaging relevance failure. The visitor clicked because of a specific offer — the page must honour it immediately. Even upselling from the ad offer (promising a free thing then immediately pushing a paid thing) breaks trust before it's established. The ad offer should be the first thing on the page, exactly as stated in the ad.
✓ Pass — offer consistent
Ad: "Free landing page audit — we'll show you exactly what it's costing you" → Page hero: "Get your free landing page audit. We show you exactly what it's costing you each month."
Exact offer, nearly exact language. Visitor's expectation immediately confirmed.
✗ Fail — offer drift
Ad: "Free CRO audit for your website" → Page: Primary CTA is "Book a £2,500 discovery workshop." Free audit mentioned in footnote.
The promised free thing is buried. Visitor immediately feels misled. Paid offer appears before the free one they came for.
Critical
16
Page opens on the customer's current reality, not the company's aspirations
The opening section mirrors the visitor's current situation or frustration before introducing the solution.
When a page opens on the visitor's own reality — "You're spending €5,000/month on ads. Most of that money is being wasted because your landing page isn't converting" — the visitor's System 1 responds with recognition: "yes, that's my situation." This creates rapport before a single claim is made. When a page opens on the company's story, it feels like a cold sales pitch starting with the seller's credentials before asking what the buyer needs.
✓ Pass — visitor-reality opening
"Your ads are running. The budget is being spent. But the conversions aren't coming in the way the numbers should allow. You've checked the targeting, the creative — but the page itself has never been properly audited."
Describes the visitor's exact experience. They feel understood before the solution is mentioned.
✗ Fail — company-first opening
"At Acme CRO, we're passionate about conversion optimisation. Our team of experts has worked with over 200 clients to deliver exceptional results using our proven methodology."
Four sentences about the company. The visitor is not mentioned until the word "clients" — as a passive object.
High
17
Industry or use-case specificity present if the ad targets a specific vertical
If the traffic source targets a specific industry, the page contains at least one explicit reference to that context.
Unity signal: a page for e-commerce that mentions "product pages" and "cart abandonment" converts better with an e-commerce audience than a generic page — even if all other copy is identical. The specific vocabulary signals that the provider has worked in this context, understands the metrics that matter, and has faced the specific problems the visitor is dealing with. This only applies when the traffic source is segmented by vertical — if the traffic is mixed, vertical-specific language will alienate non-matching visitors.
✓ Pass — vertical specific
E-commerce traffic → page mentions "product page conversion," "add-to-cart rate," "cart abandonment," and references e-commerce benchmarks (industry avg CR: 2.8% for paid search).
An e-commerce marketer reading this thinks "they know my world." Every metric is one they track daily.
✗ Fail — generic for vertical traffic
Ad targeting e-commerce managers → page talks only about "lead generation funnels," "consultation bookings," and "B2B sales pipeline." No e-commerce vocabulary anywhere.
Complete context mismatch. The visitor thinks "this isn't for me" even if the service would help them.
Medium
18
No content contradicts or undermines the ad's implicit promise
The page contains no pricing, tone, or content signals that would surprise or alarm a visitor arriving from the specific ad.
Expectation violations — even positive ones — cause cognitive friction. If the ad implies "affordable" and the page shows enterprise pricing starting at €25,000/month, the mismatch produces distrust and immediate exit. Every element of the page must be consistent with the expectations created by the ad: pricing level, visual quality, copy sophistication, and offer structure.
✓ Pass — consistent expectations
Ad targets SME budget (€1k–€5k/month ad spend) → page shows pricing of €500–€2,000/month, uses accessible language, no enterprise logos, straightforward process.
Everything confirms "this is for someone at your scale." No unexpected signals.
✗ Fail — expectation mismatch
Ad: "Affordable CRO for growing businesses" → Page shows "trusted by Nike, Amazon, HSBC" and minimum engagement of €50,000.
The logos and minimum engagement create an immediate "this isn't for me" response in the SME visitor the ad targeted.
High
19
Visual hierarchy reinforces the ad's primary message
The most prominent visual element relates directly to the ad's main claim — not to brand aesthetics alone.
Visitors scan before they read. The visual hierarchy communicates the message independently of copy — and does so faster. If the ad is about revenue recovery and the hero image shows a team meeting, the visual breaks the scent before copy is processed. Test: look at the page for 2 seconds with eyes slightly unfocused. What is the dominant message conveyed by size, colour, and positioning alone? Does that message match the ad?
✓ Pass — visually aligned
Ad about "seeing what's costing you conversions" → Hero shows an audit output screenshot with specific issues highlighted and revenue numbers attached.
The image demonstrates the exact thing the ad promised. Copy and visual tell the same story simultaneously.
✗ Fail — visual contradiction
Ad about analytical, data-driven auditing → Hero image is a soft-focus photo of two people shaking hands at a coffee shop. No data, no analysis visible anywhere.
Image communicates "relationship business" while copy says "data-driven analysis." The visitor feels the inconsistency.
Medium
20
Page load speed: under 3 seconds on mobile (PageSpeed ≥ 70)
First Contentful Paint under 3 seconds on simulated 4G. Google PageSpeed Insights mobile score ≥ 70.
Google's data: 53% of mobile visitors abandon a page that takes longer than 3 seconds to load. For paid traffic, every slow-loading visitor is a complete waste of ad spend — the person paid for, arrived, and left before seeing a single word of copy. Specific culprits: uncompressed images (use WebP, max 200KB per image), render-blocking JavaScript, Google Fonts loading, third-party scripts (analytics, chat widgets, heatmap tools all add 200–500ms each). Test at pagespeed.web.dev with the specific landing page URL — not the homepage.
✓ Pass benchmarks
PageSpeed mobile score ≥ 70. FCP < 1.8s. LCP < 2.5s. CLS < 0.1. Total page weight < 2MB.
Google's "Good" thresholds for Core Web Vitals. Meeting all four means the page doesn't haemorrhage paid traffic visitors.
✗ Fail — common causes
5MB hero image (JPG, uncompressed) · 4 third-party tracking scripts loading synchronously · Google Fonts without preconnect · YouTube embed loading full player JS on page load.
Each alone can push load time above 5 seconds. Together: 8–12 seconds. Over 80% of paid visitors gone before the page paints.
Critical
03/07 Clarity & Cognitive Ease LIFT-C · Krug · Kahneman 0/10 ▼
Is the offer, structure, and next action unambiguous? Confusion is the most invisible conversion killer — visitors rarely know they're confused. They just leave. Cognitive ease (Kahneman) means: things that are easy to process feel more true, more credible, and more desirable. Friction in reading equals friction in deciding.
21
Primary CTA is visible without scrolling on both desktop and mobile
A CTA button or form field is present above the fold — no scrolling required to find the first action opportunity.
The above-fold CTA serves two functions: (1) it captures visitors who decide immediately — the 10–20% already sold by the headline, and (2) it anchors the page's purpose so every visitor knows from the first second what action they're being invited to take. Test: load the page on a mobile device, don't scroll, and ask "can I take the primary action right now?" If no, this fails.
✓ Pass
URL input field + "Get my free audit" button visible on first load on iPhone 13 (390px viewport). No scrolling needed.
The visitor who decides in 5 seconds can act in 5 seconds. Zero friction between decision and action.
✗ Fail
Hero section: 800px tall video background with animated text that fades in sequentially. CTA appears after 4th text element at 6 seconds, positioned below the video fold.
Visitor ready to act at second 2 has no mechanism to act. By second 6 when the CTA appears, 60% of mobile visitors have already left.
Critical
22
One clearly dominant CTA — no two buttons of equal visual weight
Only one button or action is visually dominant at any point. Secondary actions are visually subordinate.
Hick's Law: decision time increases logarithmically with the number of options. Two CTAs of equal visual weight create a micro-decision the visitor didn't sign up for: "which one should I click?" This pause breaks momentum, and many visitors resolve it by clicking neither. Secondary CTAs (e.g., "Watch a 2-min demo") are acceptable but must be visually subordinate: smaller, lower contrast, text link rather than filled button.
✓ Pass — clear hierarchy
Primary: large cyan filled button "Get my free audit →" · Secondary: plain text link below "or watch a 2-minute demo" in 70% opacity grey.
Primary CTA has full visual weight. Secondary is clearly subordinate — it doesn't compete for the eye.
✗ Fail — equal weight CTAs
Two buttons side by side, identical size, identical cyan fill: "Get Free Audit" and "View Case Studies."
50% of clicks go to case studies instead of conversion. The visitor is forced to choose — a significant portion bounce rather than choose wrong.
Critical
23
No paragraph in body copy exceeds 3 sentences
Every paragraph is readable in a single breath. Wall-of-text sections are absent.
Krug's Don't Make Me Think: long paragraphs require active effort to parse. On a landing page, active effort equals cognitive friction equals motivation to leave. When visitors encounter a dense paragraph, their System 1 calculates: "the effort required to read this exceeds the likely reward." Most visitors skip dense paragraphs entirely — meaning writing them is both wasted effort and a visual deterrent that makes the page feel heavy.
✓ Pass — scannable copy
"Most landing page problems are invisible until you measure them. We audit 70 criteria and attach a revenue number to each issue. So instead of a vague recommendation list, you get a prioritised fix plan with a monthly cost per problem."
3 sentences. Each one advances the argument independently. Can be read in under 10 seconds.
✗ Fail — wall of text
A 7-sentence paragraph explaining the full methodology, history of CRO, why data matters, how the audit process was developed, what frameworks were used, and how results are typically delivered — all as one unbroken block.
Most visitors scan the first line and skip the rest. The information exists on the page but is never read.
High
24
No unexplained jargon or acronyms in above-fold copy
Every word in the headline and first screen is understandable to a non-specialist in the target audience.
Kahneman's cognitive ease: things that are hard to parse feel less true and less trustworthy. Jargon signals "designed for insiders" — which excludes anyone who doesn't already know the vocabulary, and paradoxically fails to impress even those who do (because insiders know jargon often hides imprecise thinking). Exception: specialist channels where jargon is expected vocabulary among the audience.
✓ Pass — audience-matched vocabulary
For CMO audience: "We identify conversion leaks, calculate their monthly revenue cost, and give you a prioritised fix plan backed by your own traffic data."
No unexplained jargon. "Conversion leaks" explained by context. Any marketing professional gets this instantly.
✗ Fail — unexplained acronyms
Hero for general business audience: "Optimise your CVR using LIFT-model CRO methodology aligned with your ROAS targets and CAC/LTV ratio."
Six acronyms in one sentence for an audience that likely knows two or three of them. The others create confusion and signal insider exclusivity.
High
25
Page has one primary goal — not multiple competing conversion objectives
All roads lead to one action. The page does not simultaneously try to generate leads, sell a product, build a newsletter, and book a call.
The leaky bucket principle: every additional goal on a page reduces conversion toward the primary goal. Each goal requires different emotional positioning, different proof, different CTA language. A page trying to do all of them does none well. Test: "what is the one thing I want every visitor to do?" If you can't name one thing, the page has too many goals.
✓ Pass — single goal
Every CTA on the page says some variation of "get the free audit." The about section links back to the audit CTA. The proof section ends with the audit CTA. One destination, multiple paths to it.
Multiple CTAs are fine when they all lead to the same place. The goal is singular; the access points are multiple.
✗ Fail — goal proliferation
Above fold: "Book a consultation." Mid-page: "Download our free guide." Below proof: "Follow us on LinkedIn." Footer: "Subscribe to our newsletter." Also: "Shop our templates."
5 different goals competing on one page. The primary goal gets roughly 20% of the attention it would get alone.
Critical
26
Process explained in 3–4 steps maximum
If the page includes a "how it works" section, it contains maximum 4 steps, with the first being minimal effort.
StoryBrand: a plan with 3 steps removes decision paralysis. The plan section exists to answer "what happens if I click?" — not to document the full delivery process. More than 4 steps introduces process anxiety: the visitor imagines the commitment required and retreats. Step 1 must be as effortless as possible: "Send us your URL" requires no thinking, no preparation, no vulnerability.
✓ Pass — 3-step process
1. Send us your URL (10 seconds) → 2. We audit across 70 criteria and calculate your revenue leak (48 hours) → 3. We walk you through the findings on a free 20-minute call.
Step 1 is trivially easy. Each step is described from the visitor's perspective. Time commitments are stated.
✗ Fail — 8-step process
8-step onboarding: Discovery call → Briefing document → Kick-off meeting → Audit phase 1 → Mid-point review → Audit phase 2 → Report preparation → Findings presentation.
The visitor's reaction: "I need to attend 3 meetings before I even know if this is worth it." Most exit rather than commit to all 8 steps.
High
27
Microcopy adjacent to the CTA explains what happens after clicking
Within 1–2 lines of the CTA button, the visitor is told exactly what the next step is.
Post-click anxiety is one of the highest-impact conversion killers that never appears in analytics because it happens before the click. "What actually happens if I click this? Am I signing up for something? Is there a credit card required? Will someone call me?" Microcopy like "Takes 60 seconds · No card required · We'll email you the report" answers three objections in 8 words. Specificity matters: "No commitment" is too vague. "No credit card — we'll email your report within 48 hours" resolves specific fears.
✓ Pass — specific microcopy
Button: "Get my free conversion audit →" · Below: "Takes 60 seconds · Free forever · We'll walk you through it on a 20-min call · No pitch · Findings are yours to keep."
Answers: how long? · what does it cost? · what happens next? · what's the catch? · what if I don't continue? Five fears resolved.
✗ Fail — absent microcopy
Button: "Book a Call" — nothing else nearby.
What kind of call? How long? Will it be a sales call? These unresolved questions prevent the click from visitors who are almost ready to convert.
High
28
Visual hierarchy guides the eye in a clear top-to-bottom narrative path
Page elements are sized and weighted so the reading order follows the persuasion sequence without visual confusion.

The word "FREE" is used explicitly if the offer is free
If the primary offer costs nothing, the CTA and adjacent copy uses the word "free" — not "$0", "no cost", or "complimentary."
Ariely's FREE! effect (Predictably Irrational, Chapter 3): the word "free" triggers an emotional response disproportionate to its rational meaning. In controlled experiments, "free chocolate" converted dramatically better than "1-cent chocolate" even though the 1-cent option was more valuable in absolute terms. The mechanism: "free" eliminates the cost-benefit calculation entirely — there is no downside to evaluate. "$0 audit" and "free audit" communicate identical information, but "free audit" produces meaningfully higher conversion because the word carries emotional charge that "$0" does not.
✓ Pass — "free" used explicitly
"Get my FREE conversion audit →" · "Free · No credit card · No commitment" · "It's free. We mean it."
The word appears multiple times, is visually prominent, and is not buried in microcopy.
✗ Fail — "free" avoided
"Get your complimentary conversion audit →" · "No-cost page analysis" · "€0 audit report"
"Complimentary" reads as formal and corporate. "No-cost" is a double negative. "€0" is purely rational and misses the emotional charge entirely. All three convert below "free" equivalents.

Hero section: "CRO Audit — €1,200. Book now." Price is the second thing on the page, before value is established.
